Why it moves gold: gold pays no interest, so the real (after-inflation) return on safe bonds is the price of holding it. When real yields fall, holding gold costs less and its price tends to rise — the two lines usually move in opposite directions. This is widely considered gold's most important driver.

Why it moves gold: gold is priced in dollars, so when the dollar strengthens, gold becomes more expensive for buyers in every other currency and demand softens — a rising dollar is usually a headwind for gold, a falling dollar a tailwind. The index shown here tracks the dollar against the euro, yen, pound, Canadian dollar, krona and franc using the same weights as the well-known DXY index (computed from ECB reference rates).

3 · Speculative positioning …

Loading CFTC data…

Why it moves gold: every week the CFTC publishes how hedge funds ("managed money") are positioned in gold futures. Rising net longs add fuel to rallies — but extreme positioning works in reverse: when nearly everyone is already long, there are few buyers left and the market becomes vulnerable to sharp washouts; when longs have been flushed out, gold often finds a bottom. Traders therefore read this as a contrarian gauge at the extremes. The percentile compares today's net position with the past three years of weekly readings.
